CHASING CHAOS
A Documentary Series
Six-Episode Series Bible — First Draft
SERIES OVERVIEW
Chasing Chaos is a six-episode documentary series following a group of men and women bound together not by sport but by survival. They met in the rooms of Alcoholics Anonymous and Narcotics Anonymous, in church basements and hospital corridors, in the quiet wreckage that follows a life lived at full throttle in the wrong direction. Now, they are attempting to conquer the Baja 1000, the world's most brutal and punishing off-road race, a 1,000-mile gauntlet through the Sonoran Desert that has broken professional teams with unlimited budgets and decades of experience.
These are not professional racers. They are a venture capitalist who grew up in the circus. A former U.S. Special Forces medic who built a fifty-million-dollar company from nothing. A man who manufactured illegal firearms, went to federal prison, cooperated with the government, and now quietly runs a mental health operation while working covert intelligence. A recovering cocaine addict who relapsed, overdosed, and was brought back to life with Narcan. A three-hundred-pound spiritual giant who drinks a case of beer a day and wears no shoes. A seasoned desert veteran in his mid-sixties who has been navigating Baja since the late 1980s and has seen things out there that would make most men walk away. A doctor who is also a love interest, navigating her own sense of belonging. A childhood friend carrying the weight of mental illness. And a business partner whose Instagram presence is significantly more polished than his self-awareness.
The Baja 1000 is the frame. Redemption is the story.
The series operates in the tradition of Gold Rush Alaska in its structure and sensibility. It is grounded in the real, the chaotic, the unscripted. There are no fabricated conflicts because the conflicts are already written into the DNA of every person on this team. There is no manufactured drama because every one of these individuals carries enough drama in their past to fuel a dozen television series. The cameras simply have to show up and stay present. The series will be raw, intimate, cinematic when it earns it, quiet when the silence speaks louder than words, and relentlessly honest about what it means to be a human being trying not to fall apart while attempting something that has no business being attempted.
The race itself is a metaphor that the series never has to explain. It is already there, embedded in every mile of the Baja peninsula. The desert does not forgive. It does not care about your recovery chips or your net worth or your AA chip count. It will break your vehicle and strand you in the dark and test every single thing you think you know about yourself and the people beside you. And that, for these men and women, is exactly why they signed up.
Each episode is 45 minutes in length. The series is six episodes total.
The tone is that of Gold Rush Alaska in its bones but with something Gold Rush never quite had: the willingness to go emotionally deep. This series will sit alongside the mechanical chaos and the logistical disasters and the screaming engines with genuine moments of confession, vulnerability, and grace. It will be both gritty and tender. Both loud and still.
SECTION ONE: CHARACTER PROFILES
CHARACTER ONE: BRETT
Full Name: Brett
Age: 36
Role in Team: Team Leader, Primary Investor, Chief Decision Maker
Professional Life: Venture Capital Partner, Clinic Investor
Hometown: Fort Worth, Texas
Background and Biography
Brett is the gravitational center of everything that follows. Without Brett there is no team, no race, no story. He funds the operation, organizes the logistics at the highest level, and sets the cultural and emotional tone of the entire enterprise. When Brett speaks, people listen. Not because he demands it, but because he has earned it in ways that are difficult to articulate until you spend time with him.
He grew up in Fort Worth in a military household, the kind of upbringing that values discipline, structure, sacrifice, and loyalty above almost everything else. His father served. The expectation of service, in some form, was embedded early. But Brett, even as a young man, was drawn to something looser, something freer, something that existed outside the clean lines of military order.
He joined the carnival. Not as a metaphor. Literally. As a young man, Brett found himself traveling with a carnival operation across the United States, working in an environment that was chaotic, colourful, itinerant, and almost entirely without structure. The carnival attracted people on the margins of conventional life, people who, for various reasons, could not or would not conform to the standard American trajectory. Brett fit in there in ways he probably did not fully understand at the time.
And it was within that world, moving from town to town, living in the rhythm of the road and the noise of midways and the company of people who had very little in common with the world he came from, that his relationship with alcohol deepened into something darker than recreation. The carnival life, for all its freedom, carried with it a culture of drinking that was structural rather than incidental. It was how the day ended. It was how people connected. It was how you got through the boredom between the towns. And for Brett, it became something he needed.
By the time he was in his late twenties, he was deep inside addiction. The details of what it looked like at its worst are his to share on camera, but the shape of it was familiar: escalating dependency, deteriorating circumstances, a growing gap between the person he knew he was and the person his drinking was making him. He sought help. He found a mentor within the recovery community who became instrumental in his getting sober. That relationship, the idea of a more experienced person anchoring a younger one who is struggling, would become a template Brett returned to again and again in his own life as he built something new.
After leaving the carnival behind and coming into sobriety, Brett educated himself, built connections, and eventually became a partner in a venture capital firm. He also invested in and developed a portfolio of clinics, the specifics of which will be defined further in production, but which appear to sit at the intersection of healthcare and functional wellness. He is by any conventional measure successful, wealthy, and respected.
But what makes Brett interesting as a documentary subject is not the success. It is the particular quality of his leadership. He is not loud. He does not operate by intimidation. He commands through presence, through consistency, through the simple fact that he has done the harder thing before and people around him know it. He is the person in the room who has already thought three steps further than everyone else and is simply waiting for everyone else to catch up.
His situationship with Lauren, the team's driver and a doctor, adds a layer of emotional complexity that the series will handle with care. There is something genuine between them, but the nature of it, the uncertainty of what it is or where it is going, creates a thread of quiet tension that runs underneath the operational chaos.
The Baja 1000 for Brett is not purely a vanity project, though it is also not purely selfless. It is, at its core, an extension of the same philosophy that brought this team together: that people on the margins of conventional success, people who have fallen down in spectacular and sometimes criminal ways, deserve a structure in which to prove to themselves and the world that they are more than their worst moments. He created this team deliberately. Most of these people are in recovery. That is not a coincidence.
Camera Presence
Brett will come across on camera as measured, thoughtful, and quietly magnetic. He is not a performer. He does not play to the lens. That quality will read as authenticity. He will be at his most compelling in the moments of conflict, particularly with Mike, when the alpha-male tension between two strong, experienced men is forced into a small, hot, broken vehicle in the middle of the Sonoran Desert.
Story Arc Across the Series
Episodes one and two establish Brett as organizer, investor, and emotional anchor. Episodes three and four put him under maximum pressure when the mechanical and logistical reality of the pre-run and the race itself begin to expose the limits of even his composure. Episodes five and six bring him to a quieter, more reflective place where the questions the series has been building about why he really did this begin to find provisional answers.
CHARACTER TWO: GOLDSTEIN
Full Name: Goldstein
Age: 34
Role in Team: VC Partner, Brett's Business Associate and Understudy
Professional Life: Venture Capital, Events and Social
Hometown: Austin, Texas
Background and Biography
Goldstein is Brett's business partner and, in many respects, Brett's shadow. He is younger, looser, more socially performative, and significantly more invested in the idea of being seen doing interesting things than in the quiet work of actually doing them. This is not said cruelly. It is said honestly, because it is what makes him interesting on camera.
He is also in recovery from alcohol and drug addiction. He and Brett met in Alcoholics Anonymous, which is the origin story of their professional relationship and their friendship. That two men who met in the rooms of AA went on to build a venture capital partnership together says something about both of them. Recovery, for both, was not a destination but a beginning.
In Austin, Goldstein has built a visible social presence. He runs events and parties. He is a mild socialite in the Austin scene. He is the kind of person who photographs well and moves easily through rooms. His Instagram presence, which is polished and consistent, stands in contrast to the deeper, messier work of the inner life that brought him to AA in the first place.
His relationship with Brett is layered. He is genuinely Brett's understudy in the professional sense, relying on Brett's experience and instincts in the VC world. But there is also affection there, and loyalty, and the particular bond that forms between two people who have seen each other at their most vulnerable and chose to stay. Within the team, Goldstein occupies a slightly uncertain position. He is not a mechanic. He is not a logistician. He is Brett's person, which gives him status without giving him a particularly clear role.
This uncertainty, played against the chaos and the very real demands of the Baja environment, will create interesting television. Goldstein will want to contribute. He will not always know how. He will manage that tension in the way that his personality dictates, which is socially, externally, through the cultivation of moments that look good rather than through the grinding internal work that the desert will actually require.
The series should treat him with affection rather than contempt. He is genuinely likeable. He is also, in ways that the cameras will reveal simply by staying present, still doing the work of figuring out who he is when no one is watching.
Camera Presence
Goldstein will be entertaining on camera. He has the social fluency of someone who has spent years in rooms full of strangers. He will be quotable. He will also have moments where the performance slips and something more real and more uncertain comes through. Those moments are the ones the series will want to find and keep.
Story Arc Across the Series
Goldstein functions as a contrast to Brett throughout the series. Where Brett is earned, Goldstein is still earning. His arc, if the series does its job, is one of gradual authenticity. The desert will strip the Instagram performance away, and what is underneath will be genuinely moving to watch.
CHARACTER THREE: MIKE NEMKE
Full Name: Mike Nemke
Age: 42
Role in Team: Mechanic, Logistics, Medical
Professional Life: Founder of a Functional Health Company, net worth approximately $50 million
Hometown: Austin, Texas
Background and Biography
Mike Nemke is the most immediately intense person on this team, and this is a team that contains a former federal prisoner who worked covert intelligence and a man with a Narcan revival in his recent history. Mike is an alpha in the purest sense of the word, not because he performs dominance but because he genuinely operates at a level that is several registers above most people in terms of focus, discipline, and the willingness to push himself and everyone around him past the point where most people would stop.
His childhood was hard in ways that leave marks. Multiple foster homes. A childhood without the kind of stable, consistent attachment that most people take for granted and that, when it is absent, shapes everything that comes after in ways that are both obvious and subtle. Mike did not come from resources or networks or family connections. What he came from was himself, and he built everything from that.
He served in the United States Special Forces as a medic. That background informs almost everything about how he moves through the world. He is trained to stay calm in chaos, to make rapid decisions with incomplete information, to endure physical and psychological stress that would break most people. He has also been shaped by the particular culture of elite military service, which is demanding, insular, intensely hierarchical in some ways and fiercely egalitarian in others, and which leaves its participants with both extraordinary capabilities and particular blind spots.
After leaving the military, Mike channelled that intensity into building a functional health business that has made him, by any measure, wealthy and successful. He has won at almost everything he has directed his energy toward. But, as the brief notes with clarity, that comes with a tax. His personal life and his mental health have paid the price for the relentlessness with which he operates. The details of that will emerge through the cameras over the course of the series.
In the context of the team, Mike's relationship with Brett is the most volatile and the most interesting dynamic the series has to offer. Two alpha males, both accustomed to being the most capable person in the room, both with strong views about how things should be done, both with the kind of ego that comes from having built something significant from almost nothing. When things go wrong with the car, which they will, and when the pressure of the desert and the race compress the space between them, the resulting conflict will be genuine, unscripted, and entirely compelling.
Camera Presence
Mike will be magnetic and unnerving in roughly equal measure. He is the kind of person who makes the air in a room feel different when he walks into it. He is not performative. He will be deeply uncomfortable with anything that feels dishonest or staged, which will work in the series' favour because it will keep every interaction with him real. His most revealing moments will probably not be in the big confrontational scenes but in the quiet ones, when the cameras catch him in the desert at night, or alone with a mechanical problem, or in a moment of unguarded reflection.
Story Arc Across the Series
Mike is the series' most compelling secondary character. His arc is about the cost of being built the way he is built. The Baja race is not, for him, a challenge in the way it is for the others. It is just another arena in which to be excellent. The drama of his journey is what happens when excellence is not enough, when the desert laughs at your discipline and your net worth and your Special Forces background and strands you in the dark with a broken axle and a man you're about to lose your temper with.
CHARACTER FOUR: JEFFREY
Full Name: Jeffrey
Age: 42
Role in Team: Mechanic
Professional Life: Owner of Several Ketamine Clinics, Counter Intelligence
Hometown: Utah
Background and Biography
Jeffrey is the most layered and the most extraordinary character in a group of extraordinary characters, and that is saying a great deal. He is also the one whose story the series will have to navigate most carefully, both in terms of what can be said legally and what can be said ethically, and what, once said, will land with the audience as the genuine revelation it is.
He manufactured firearms and sold them outside the United States to splinter groups. He was prosecuted federally. He did time in federal prison. He then cooperated with the government, negotiated a reduced sentence, and built a new life in Utah with his family.
He was addicted to methamphetamine from the age of nineteen to the age of twenty-four. That is five years of addiction to one of the most destructive substances in the modern pharmacopeia, during which time he was also, apparently, conducting the kind of illegal arms operations that most people would not attempt while entirely sober. The combination of high-level operational intelligence and severe addiction is, in Jeffrey's case, not a contradiction but almost an explanation. He is a person of extraordinary cognitive capacity who, in the absence of appropriate structure and direction, directed that capacity toward destruction.
He is now, the brief tells us, one of the most remarkable personalities these filmmakers have ever encountered. He runs ketamine clinics, which sit in the emerging and genuinely fascinating space of psychedelic-assisted mental health treatment. He works as a counter-intelligence agent within the United States government. He lives quietly in Utah with his family. He is a man who has been through the most extreme version of multiple American experiences, crime, prison, addiction, federal cooperation, reintegration, and has come out the other side not broken but, improbably, whole. Or something approaching whole. The mental health challenges referenced in his brief suggest that the wholeness is real but hard-won and ongoing.
His presence on this team is significant. He is not here for clout or for content. He is here because Brett recruited him specifically, and Brett recruits specifically. What Jeffrey represents is the most extreme version of the team's central thesis: that the worst thing you have done does not have to be the defining thing about you.
Camera Presence
Jeffrey will be a gift to the cameras if he chooses to be present with them. His intelligence, the way he thinks and the way he speaks, will be unlike anything else in the series. He will be precise, layered, occasionally cryptic, and genuinely funny in a dark, dry way that very intelligent people often are. The challenge will be getting him to open up, because a man with his history has very good reasons to be guarded. When the guard comes down, even partially, the series will have some of its best moments.
Story Arc Across the Series
Jeffrey's arc is about the question of identity after reinvention. Can you genuinely become someone new? Is the person who did those things the same person who is standing in the desert trying to rebuild a suspension component? The series will not answer that question for him. It will simply keep the cameras running and let him live it.
CHARACTER FIVE: RON
Full Name: Ron
Age: 36
Role in Team: Mechanic Support
Professional Life: Tech
Hometown: Austin, Texas
Background and Biography
Ron is the team's most uncertain figure, and the brief is admirably honest about this. He is described as a clout-chasing, overly agreeable sidekick to Mike whose calm reads as performative and whose focus appears to be more on Instagram relevance than on substance. The brief notes explicitly that he feels temporary and disposable, and that his future involvement in the series is unclear.
In the documentary tradition, Ron represents a type. He is the hanger-on, the person who has attached himself to a more powerful and more defined personality and is using that proximity as a substitute for his own identity. This is not unusual. It is, in fact, a very recognizable human behaviour. What makes it interesting on camera is the contrast between his presentation and the reality of what the desert demands.
The Baja 1000 environment is specifically brutal to people who are performing rather than being. It strips performance away. It will be interesting to observe what, if anything, remains underneath Ron's curated calm when the car breaks down at mile 600 and it is three in the morning and the nearest support vehicle is forty minutes away.
Camera Presence
Ron will be likeable on camera in the way that people who have practised being likeable are always likeable. The question is whether there is anything beneath the likability that the series can find and use. His uncertain future involvement suggests the production team is already asking this question.
Story Arc Across the Series
Ron's arc, if he remains in the series, is the arc of the person who discovers that performance is insufficient. Whether that discovery leads anywhere meaningful will depend on what Ron does with it.
CHARACTER SIX: KENNY
Full Name: Kenny
Age: 65
Role in Team: Logistics Mechanic, Baja Veteran
Hometown: Unspecified
Background and Biography
Kenny is the series' wisest presence and, in many ways, its moral compass. He has been navigating the Baja 1000 since the late 1980s. That means he has been in this desert, with these machines, through this chaos, for nearly forty years. He has seen everything. He has survived everything. And the particular kind of calm that emerges from that depth of experience is something that no amount of money or intelligence or athletic conditioning can replicate or rush.
Brett employs him specifically to navigate the logistics of the race operation, which is a sophisticated and demanding task that requires not just knowledge of the course but relationships with the local support networks, understanding of where things go wrong and where they go catastrophically wrong, and the ability to communicate all of that to a group of men who have never done this before and who believe, with the particular confidence of the self-made, that their various forms of excellence will translate to this environment.
Kenny knows they won't. At least not automatically. And he knows it not from reading about it but from watching it happen, year after year, to team after team.
He is camera-shy, the brief tells us. That quality, in a documentary series full of people who are, in various ways, performing, will be extraordinarily valuable. The camera gravitates toward authenticity, and Kenny's discomfort with being watched will paradoxically make him one of the most watchable people in the series. His few words will carry more weight than most people's many.
He is described as a survivor. The specifics of what he has survived are not detailed in the brief, and that ambiguity is probably appropriate. Kenny is not the kind of person who explains himself. He is the kind of person who simply is what he is, and what he is is rock solid.
Camera Presence
Kenny will be the series' steadiest presence and one of its most quietly compelling. The moments between Kenny and the younger, louder, more volatile members of the team will be among the richest the series produces. When Kenny speaks, the editing should let the words land without rushing past them.
Story Arc Across the Series
Kenny's arc is not about transformation. He has already transformed. His arc is about transmission, the passing of something hard-earned from a man who has lived it to a group of people who are only beginning to understand what they have signed up for.
CHARACTER SEVEN: TYLER
Full Name: Tyler
Age: 36
Role in Team: Mechanic
Professional Life: Family Business, Fort Worth
Hometown: Fort Worth, Texas
Background and Biography
Tyler is Brett's childhood friend, which makes him the person on this team with the longest history with the project's central figure, and that history gives him a particular kind of access and a particular kind of authority that is informal but real. He has known Brett before the money, before the sobriety, before the VC firm, before the Baja idea. He knew Brett when.
He works in the family business in Fort Worth, which roots him in a world that is distinct from the Austin VC scene that surrounds most of the rest of the team. He is not wealthy in the way Brett and Mike are wealthy. He is not connected in the way Goldstein is connected. He is just a man from Fort Worth who can fix a chassis in two hours and who Brett trusts completely.
He is going through significant mental health challenges. The brief is deliberately unspecific about the nature of these challenges, and the series should follow suit until Tyler chooses, on camera, to be more specific. What is clear is that he is carrying something heavy, and that the Baja adventure, for Tyler, is happening alongside that weight rather than as an escape from it.
He can be cold with people he does not trust. He is warm with people he does. The cameras, if they are patient and respectful, will earn his warmth. And when they do, the series will have access to one of its most honest and most grounded perspectives on what Brett is actually like as a person and a friend over the long term.
He is described as the ultimate team player. He will work without complaint. He will do the unglamorous jobs. He will be there when the others have gone to sleep. That kind of person is the backbone of any real operation, and the series should honour that.
Camera Presence
Tyler will be initially guarded with the cameras. That guardedness is itself interesting. As the series progresses and his comfort with the production grows, his willingness to open up about the mental health struggles and his long friendship with Brett will yield some of the series' most quietly powerful moments.
Story Arc Across the Series
Tyler's arc is about showing up anyway. Carrying what you carry and showing up anyway. The series will find in him a kind of grace that the louder, more dramatic figures cannot quite access.
CHARACTER EIGHT: LAUREN
Full Name: Lauren
Age: 32
Role in Team: Driver
Professional Life: Doctor
Hometown: Canandaigua (approximately, per brief)
Background and Biography
Lauren is the series' most complex female presence and its only driver, which gives her a role that is simultaneously central and contested. She earned a medical degree by an unconventional path, which suggests she is someone accustomed to doing things differently and to proving herself in environments that were not designed with her in mind.
Her relationship with Brett is described as a situationship, which is a word that carries a great deal of modern cultural weight and which the series should not shy away from. They are something, but the nature of that something is undefined, which creates a particular kind of tension and a particular kind of vulnerability on both sides. Lauren is drawn to Brett, but the brief also notes that she seems more drawn to being close to Brett than to the chaos of the enterprise itself, at least initially.
She felt overwhelmed by the intensity at first. That is honest and important. She is the character in this story who most represents the audience's likely position: arriving in a world that is loud and dangerous and full of alpha energy and finding it, at least initially, too much. Her growth into the team, which the brief suggests will happen over time, is a genuine arc that the series can track and earn.
She is kind but quick to judge, which is an interesting combination. It suggests someone who processes the world rapidly and whose first assessments tend to stick. She will need the series to give her enough time to revise those first assessments, both of the people around her and of her own capacity for this particular kind of chaos.
Camera Presence
Lauren will be the series' most relatable presence for a significant portion of the audience. Her outsider perspective on the team will provide some of the series' sharpest observational moments. As she grows into her role as driver, that perspective will shift, and the series should capture that shift carefully.
Story Arc Across the Series
Lauren's arc is about belonging. About earning the thing you want to be part of rather than simply being near it. Her most compelling moments will come when she is behind the wheel, because driving is where the performance of warmth and the performance of competence align with the actual work, and the desert has no interest in anything that is not the actual work.
CHARACTER NINE: TOMMY
Full Name: Tommy
Age: 31
Role in Team: Head of Logistics
Professional Life: Works directly for Brett
Hometown: Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania
Background and Biography
Tommy is the series' emotional centre and its most urgent story. Everything else in Chasing Chaos is compelling. Tommy is necessary. Without Tommy, the series is a very good documentary about broken men trying to fix themselves through adventure. With Tommy, it is something else entirely.
He is a recovering cocaine addict and a serial relapser. He relapsed after the team returned from Baja in December. Not long after. The emotional whiplash of that timing, the hope that the desert generated and the speed with which that hope was overwhelmed by the addiction, is the series' most devastating beat.
He overdosed. He was revived with Narcan. He is now in rehab. He is hoping to return in April.
Tommy is why Brett built this team. The series can say that clearly, and it should. Brett did not assemble this group of recovering addicts and ex-convicts and mental health strugglers because he thought it would make good television. He assembled them because he believes in a particular model of recovery, one that involves purpose and brotherhood and something to work toward that is bigger than the individual, and Tommy is the clearest expression of that belief and the clearest test of its limits.
Tommy will not always be present in the series. Some episodes will have him only in conversation, only in Brett's phone calls, only in the worry lines around Brett's eyes when someone mentions his name. That absence, and the specific quality of the worry it generates, will be as powerful as his presence.
When he is present, Tommy will be the series' most open and most unguarded voice. Addiction and near-death experience tend to do that to people. They strip away the performances that the world asks you to maintain and leave something more raw and more real.
His arc is the series' most explicit redemption narrative. Whether it resolves cleanly or messily or not at all is not something the series can control, and that unpredictability is exactly what makes it essential television.
Camera Presence
Tommy will likely be the most emotionally affecting person on camera in the entire series. Not because he performs emotion but because he has very little capacity left for not feeling things. The series will need to handle his story with care and with genuine respect for his dignity, and also with the honesty that he himself, by all accounts, brings to his own story.
Story Arc Across the Series
Tommy's arc is the series' spine. It begins before episode one, in the overdose and the Narcan and the rehab. It runs through the series as absence and concern. And it arrives, either in triumph or in the more complicated truth that recovery does not follow narrative conventions, at the series' end.
CHARACTER TEN: FOSS
Full Name: Foss
Age: Unspecified
Role in Team: New Participant, Major Character
Background: Family connected to Spindletop
Background and Biography
Foss is, by Brett's own description, a major major character. He is three hundred pounds. He drinks a case of Michelob Ultra a day. He wears no shoes. He is, despite or perhaps because of all of this, deeply spiritually aware in a way that requires no work, no practice, no formal tradition, and no obvious effort. He is described as being late in the karmic cycle, which is a phrase worth sitting with.
The Spindletop connection places his family in the history of Texas oil, which is both a story of extraordinary wealth and a story of the particular American mythology that surrounds the discovery and extraction of that wealth. Foss, in his current state, appears to be something of a departure from that mythology.
He is one of the most inherently cinematic figures in the series, not because he is performing anything but because he simply is what he is with complete and total commitment. A barefoot, beer-drinking, three-hundred-pound spiritual presence in the middle of the Sonoran Desert is, objectively, extraordinary television. The cameras will find him impossible to ignore. He will likely say things that are confusing, profound, and funny in ways that are difficult to categorize. He is the series' wildcard and possibly its most unexpected emotional gift.
Camera Presence
Foss will be completely himself on camera because he appears to be incapable of being anything else. That quality is worth more than any amount of media training.
Story Arc Across the Series
Foss's arc is the series' most open and most unpredictable thread. He arrives as a new participant and will find his own level within the group. The cameras should simply follow him and see where he goes.
CHARACTER ELEVEN: RAZ AND JOSHUA
These two new participants are introduced into the team alongside Foss. Their full biographies are to be developed as production deepens. They will be integrated into the episode-by-episode breakdowns at appropriate moments and their specific roles within the team structure will be defined in subsequent drafts. What is clear is that they represent additional layers of complexity and perspective within a group that is already extraordinarily rich.
SECTION TWO: EPISODE-BY-EPISODE SYNOPSIS
EPISODE ONE: "We All Know What We're Running From"
Runtime: 45 minutes
Cold Open
The episode begins in near-silence. Close-up on a pair of hands. The hands are large, slightly rough, the hands of a man who has done physical work. They are resting on a table. Still. The sound underneath is barely there, just the ambient hum of a room, maybe a fan, maybe traffic outside. A breath. Long and slow. Controlled, the way a person breathes who has learned to use breath as an anchor.
This is Brett. The camera pulls back slowly over perhaps fifteen seconds, revealing a man at what appears to be a kitchen table, in the early morning, in the particular quiet that exists before the day begins and after the mind has already been running for an hour. The light is thin and grey. He does not look at the camera. He looks at something just past it.
Title card: CHASING CHAOS.
Act One: Assembly
The episode's primary narrative task is introduction. We need to understand who these people are and how they found each other, and we need to understand it in the way that documentary does best, not through exposition but through the accumulation of moments.
We begin with Brett on his home turf in the Fort Worth-Austin corridor, in the world of the venture capital firm, taking calls, moving through meetings, occupying the particular kind of professional space that he has built from almost nothing. The sequence here should establish his competence and his authority, but also, through the right moments, the slight remove he keeps from the world he operates in. He is good at this world. He is not entirely of it.
Cut to a recovery meeting. Not exploitatively. Not with identifying faces or real names or location markers that could harm anyone's anonymity. But the room itself, the chairs, the coffee, the light, the particular quality of honesty that fills those rooms, is important visual context. It is where most of this team met. It should be in the series, handled with care.
We meet Goldstein in Austin. He is in a setting that suits him, something social, a brunch, a rooftop, a gathering. He is at ease. He is performing ease, which looks almost identical to feeling it. He talks about Brett. His admiration is genuine and his dependence on Brett's framing is also genuine, and the cameras should catch both.
We meet Tommy early, because Tommy's situation, as it exists at the time of filming, will require the series to address it directly and honestly. He is in rehab. His first appearance should be a phone call, Brett on one end, Tommy's voice on the other, with the camera on Brett's face. Whatever Brett's expression does during that call will be more revealing than anything either of them says.
We meet Tyler. He is in Fort Worth. He is in a garage or a workshop, doing something mechanical, already slightly turned away from the camera. He talks about Brett with the particular affection of someone who knew you before you were anyone.
Kenny appears briefly in this episode, a glimpse, a voice, a presence. He is introduced but not yet explained. He will explain himself over time, and that patience will pay off.
Act Two: The Idea

SECTION THREE: PRODUCTION FRAMEWORK, THEMATIC ARCHITECTURE, AND SERIES INTENTIONS
Tone and Stylistic Reference
The closest structural reference for Chasing Chaos is Gold Rush Alaska, as noted in the brief. Gold Rush built its audience through the combination of operational stakes, genuine human drama, and the particular texture of difficult work in difficult environments. It respected its subjects. It followed them into real situations and stayed present through the real consequences of those situations. It did not manufacture conflict because the work itself generated more genuine conflict than any production could invent.
Chasing Chaos should operate from the same philosophical foundation. The cameras follow. The cameras stay. The cameras do not intervene except in cases of genuine safety concern, and even then with the lightest possible touch. The subjects have agreed to be present with these cameras, and the series honours that agreement by being honest about what it finds.
The tonal difference from Gold Rush is the emotional depth that this series is both required and committed to reaching. Gold Rush is about men who want to find gold. Chasing Chaos is about men and women who want to find themselves, and who have chosen an extraordinarily difficult physical challenge as the arena in which to do it. That interior dimension, the recovery journeys, the mental health struggles, the histories of addiction and crime and trauma and reinvention, requires a documentary approach that is willing to go quiet, willing to sit with someone for a long time in silence, willing to let the tears happen without cutting away.
The series should feel like something you would watch on a premium streaming service while also feeling completely real. Cinematic when it earns the cinema. Raw when the rawness is what is true.
The Baja 1000 as Metaphor
The series never has to explain its central metaphor because the metaphor explains itself. The Baja 1000 is a race through the world's most unforgiving terrain in which the vehicles break down, the navigation goes wrong, the night makes everything uncertain, the support systems are stretched to their limits, and the teams that finish are not necessarily the fastest but the ones that held together when everything else fell apart.
That is also the story of recovery. That is also the story of every person on this team.
The desert does not need to be characterized as a metaphor for the production team. It simply needs to be shown honestly, and the audience will do the rest of the work.
The Role of Addiction and Recovery
The series must handle the addiction and recovery elements with both honesty and care. These are real people with real histories who have made themselves genuinely vulnerable by agreeing to be part of this project. The recovery community operates on principles of anonymity that should be respected in the ways they can be respected while still telling the story that needs to be told.
The key principle is that addiction and recovery are shown in their full complexity, not as linear journeys from darkness into light, not as cautionary tales, not as inspirational content in the performative sense, but as the actual, ongoing, non-linear, deeply human processes that they are. Tommy's relapse is not a failure. It is a moment in a longer story. Jeffrey's past is not his entirety. It is a chapter. Brett's sobriety is not a completed project. It is a daily practice.
The series should communicate all of this not through voice-over explanation but through the accumulation of honest moments.
The Role of the Desert and the Race
The mechanical and operational content of the series, the vehicle preparation, the pre-run, the race itself, should be treated with the same seriousness and respect that Gold Rush gives to its mining operations. This is real work. The stakes are real. The expertise is real. The viewers who come to this series for the racing content should find that content genuinely satisfying.
Kenny is the series' guide in this domain. His knowledge of the Baja should be treated as the deep and hard-earned expertise that it is. His guidance should be shown as valuable even when the more forceful personalities on the team resist or override it.
The vehicle should be a character. Its condition, at any given moment, is a direct reflection of the team's condition. When it runs well, the team is functioning. When it breaks down, something in the human system has also broken down. This is not metaphorical. It is mechanical and psychological at the same time.
The Brotherhood Thesis
Brett's central intention in assembling this team was, it appears, to create a structure in which people who have been through serious adversity could find something to work toward together. The race is the frame. The brotherhood is the substance.
The series should pay close attention to the ways in which that brotherhood manifests and the ways in which it struggles. The genuine moments of loyalty and support and care between these men, in the exhaustion and the chaos of the desert, will be among the series' most powerful content. The conflicts between them, when they happen, should be shown not as failures of the brotherhood thesis but as the honest stress-testing of it.
Tommy is the most explicit version of this thesis. He is the person the system was most designed to help. The fact that he relapsed and overdosed and is now in rehab is not a disproof of Brett's thesis. It is a more complicated version of it. And it is exactly what the series is for.
Foss as Spiritual Anchor
Foss's role in the series is harder to define than anyone else's, which is appropriate because Foss himself appears to resist definition. He is the series' spiritual wildcard, the presence that doesn't fit any of the established categories and therefore illuminates all of them by contrast.
His three hundred pounds and his bare feet and his daily case of beer and his Spindletop family and his deep spiritual awareness without effort are not contradictions to be resolved. They are a portrait of someone who has arrived, through whatever path, at a kind of foundational acceptance of reality as it is. That quality, in a series full of people who are working very hard to accept reality, is potentially the most interesting and most mysterious thing the cameras will find.
He should be followed with curiosity and without judgment and the cameras should give his statements the space they need to breathe and land.
New Participants Integration
Raz, Joshua, and Foss arrive as new participants in the team's story. Their integration into the group dynamics will be one of the series' interesting ongoing threads. How does an established group of people, many of whom share a deep recovery bond, absorb new members? What does it require? What does it reveal about the group's capacity for expansion versus its instinct toward insularity?
These questions should be allowed to answer themselves through footage rather than through any form of narration.
Series Arc Overview
Episode one establishes the world and the people and the question.
Episode two enters the desert for the first time and encounters the gap between the idea and the reality.
Episode three goes deeper into both the mechanical and the personal and finds the first genuine cracks.
Episode four is the race itself, the maximum-stakes version of everything the series has been building toward.
Episode five processes the aftermath and reaches the series' emotional peak.
Episode six returns to ordinary life with new eyes and asks what comes next.
The series arc moves from assembly through testing through crisis through reckoning through return. It is the shape of recovery itself. It is the shape of the Baja 1000. It is the shape of any human story that is worth telling.
On the Handling of Difficult Material
The series contains within it several stories that are, individually, extraordinary documentary material. Federal prison. Addiction. Overdose. Mental illness. Military trauma. Foster care. Firearms trafficking. Narcan revival. These are not sensational elements to be deployed for shock value. They are the actual histories of actual people who are still living and who have agreed to be present with these cameras.
The guiding principle for handling all of this material is respect. Respect for the dignity of the person whose story it is. Respect for the complexity of the experience. Respect for the audience's capacity to handle difficult material without the series having to simplify it or soften it or surround it with reassuring framing.
The stories should be told because they are true and because they matter and because the people who lived them deserve to have them witnessed.
And the series should remember, in every editorial decision, that these people are not defined by the worst thing that has ever happened to them or the worst thing they have ever done. They are defined by the whole of their lives, including the part that put them in the Sonoran Desert in a buggy, trying to make it to the finish line.
Production Note on Tommy
Tommy's story is ongoing at the time of writing this document. He is in rehab. He is hoping to return in April. The series must be prepared for multiple versions of his story's continuation. If he returns, healthy and present, his return to the team is one of the series' most important narrative moments and must be documented with full attention. If his recovery continues to be non-linear, that non-linearity is also the story, and it should be treated with the same honesty and the same respect as the more hopeful version.
The series does not have a predetermined ending for Tommy. It has a commitment to honesty about whatever his ending turns out to be.
Final Statement on the Series
Chasing Chaos is a series about people who have been to the edge of their own lives and come back. It is about what they found there and what they brought back with them. It is about the desert, which is real and beautiful and completely indifferent to human drama, and about the machines that move through it, which break and are repaired and break again. It is about brotherhood, which is real and imperfect and worth fighting for. It is about recovery, which is not a destination but a direction.
It is, in the end, about the specific miracle of people who had every reason to stop and chose, instead, to keep going.
The cameras are there to witness that. Nothing more and nothing less.
